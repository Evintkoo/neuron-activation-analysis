# Activation Cartography: Mapping Content-Type Correlates of Neuron Activation in the Tribe Simulation Model

**Date:** 2026-04-17
**Status:** Approved
**Audience:** Cross-disciplinary — accessible to technical and non-technical readers

---

## 1. Research Questions

1. Do distinct content types produce statistically distinguishable neuron activation fingerprints in the Tribe model?
2. Which theoretical framework — Dual Coding Theory, Global Workspace Theory, Predictive Coding (Free Energy Principle), or Integrated Information Theory — best predicts observed activation clustering patterns?
3. What is the marginal activation differential when content type is held constant but one semantic dimension varies (contrastive sub-study)?

---

## 2. Experimental Apparatus

The **Tribe model** (from [tribe-playground](https://github.com/Evintkoo/tribe-playground)) serves as the synthetic brain. It is an artificial neural network implemented in Rust with an interactive HTML/WASM frontend and a 3D brain mesh (`brain.obj.gz`). For this research, the model's internal neuron activations are treated as analogues to biological neural firing patterns, and activation vectors are extracted at three probe depths per forward pass.

This is an explicit modeling choice — not a claim of biological equivalence. The Tribe model is used as a controlled, inspectable simulation that allows us to test whether established neuroscientific theories have predictive power over artificial activation dynamics.

## 4. Theoretical Framework

Each framework makes falsifiable predictions about how content types should cluster activation patterns, forming the hypothesis matrix tested against experimental results.

### 4.1 Dual Coding Theory (Paivio, 1971)
Verbal and non-verbal information are processed in two independent but interconnected cognitive systems.

**Prediction:** Text stimuli and image/audio stimuli activate non-overlapping neuron clusters. Multimodal content (M4) and emotional stimuli (S3) show dual-cluster co-activation due to cross-system engagement.

### 4.2 Global Workspace Theory (Baars, 1988; Dehaene, 2011)
Salient stimuli trigger a broadcast event ("ignition") that propagates activation across the full processing network, while routine stimuli remain locally processed.

**Prediction:** Novel (B4), threatening (B3), and emotional (S3) stimuli produce broad, high-magnitude activation cascades across layers. Familiar/factual stimuli (S4) remain locally contained. Operationalized as: ignition = activation spreading to >60% of monitored neurons within `τ_steps` processing steps (configurable parameter, default = 3 layers).

### 4.3 Free Energy Principle / Predictive Coding (Friston, 2010)
The brain minimizes prediction error. Neurons fire most strongly when observed input deviates from internal predictions — i.e., when surprise is high.

**Prediction:** Novel (B4) and threatening (B3) stimuli produce the highest activation magnitude. Familiar or highly predictable stimuli (S4, M1 short-form) produce sparse, low-energy activations. This yields a direct novelty-to-activation gradient testable across all 12 categories.

### 4.4 Integrated Information Theory (Tononi, 2004 — Φ)
Consciousness and integration are proportional to Φ (phi) — the amount of information generated by a system beyond the sum of its parts.

**Prediction:** Semantically rich content (S1 narrative, B1 social) produces highly integrated, cross-cluster activation with high Φ-proxy scores. Simple modality stimuli (isolated shapes, pure tones) produce low integration.

### 4.5 Hypothesis Matrix

| Content Type | DCT | GWT | FEP | IIT |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| M1 Text/Verbal | Verbal cluster isolated | Low ignition | Low surprise | Low Φ |
| M2 Image/Visual | Visual cluster isolated | Medium ignition | Medium surprise | Medium Φ |
| M3 Audio | Non-verbal cluster isolated | Medium ignition | Medium surprise | Medium Φ |
| M4 Multimodal | Dual-cluster overlap | High ignition | Medium surprise | High Φ |
| S1 Narrative | Verbal dominant | High ignition | High surprise | Highest Φ |
| S2 Abstract | Verbal dominant | Low ignition | High surprise | Medium Φ |
| S3 Emotional | Dual-cluster overlap | High ignition | High surprise | High Φ |
| S4 Factual | Verbal dominant | Low ignition | Lowest surprise | Low Φ |
| S5 Spatial | Visual dominant | Low ignition | Low surprise | Medium Φ |
| B1 Social | Both clusters active | High ignition | High surprise | Highest Φ |
| B2 Reward | Both clusters active | Medium ignition | Medium surprise | Medium Φ |
| B3 Threat | Both clusters active | Highest ignition | Highest surprise | High Φ |
| B4 Novelty | Both clusters active | Highest ignition | Highest surprise | High Φ |

## 5. Methodology

### Phase 1 — Stimulus Construction

**Synthetic Stimuli (Controlled Baseline)**
Generate 50 stimuli per content type (600 total) as controlled, parameter-matched inputs. Rust-native generation using deterministic seeds for reproducibility.

**Real-World Datasets (Ecological Validity)**
| Category | Dataset | Size |
|---|---|---|
| M1 Text/Narrative | BookCorpus subset | 10K samples |
| M2 Image/Visual | COCO 2017 val | 5K images |
| M3 Audio | LibriSpeech test-clean | 2.6K utterances |
| S3 Emotional | AffectNet + IEMOCAP | 3K samples |
| B1 Social | Social-IQ dataset | 1.2K clips |
| B4 Novelty | ImageNet-OOD subset | 2K samples |

All preprocessing (tokenization, embedding, spectrogram conversion) implemented in Rust.

### Phase 2 — Activation Extraction

For each stimulus, run a full forward pass through the Tribe model and extract neuron firing vectors at three probe points:
- **Early layers** — low-level feature detection
- **Mid layers** — semantic integration
- **Late layers** — output representation

Each stimulus produces activation vector `A ∈ ℝⁿ` where `n` = number of monitored neurons. The full experiment produces activation tensor `M ∈ ℝ^(stimuli × neurons × layers)`, stored as binary `.bin` files per content type.

### Phase 3 — Fingerprint Generation

1. **Dimensionality reduction:** PCA → UMAP on `M` per layer, producing 2D/3D activation landscapes
2. **Clustering:** k-means (k=12) + hierarchical agglomerative clustering for emergent groupings
3. **Fingerprint:** Each content type's centroid + variance envelope in reduced space
4. **Separability scoring:** Silhouette coefficient per cluster (target: >0.6)

Implemented using the `linfa` crate for clustering and `ndarray` for matrix operations.

### Phase 4 — Theory Fit Scoring

Define a scoring function `[0, 1]` per framework per content type:

- **DCT Score:** Overlap coefficient between verbal-dominant and visual-dominant cluster centroids. Low overlap = high DCT fit for modality-separated types.
- **GWT Ignition Score:** Fraction of neurons activated above threshold τ per stimulus. High spread in B3/B4 = high GWT fit.
- **FEP Surprise Score:** Spearman correlation between novelty rating and activation magnitude across all stimuli. Positive correlation = high FEP fit.
- **IIT Φ-Proxy:** Mutual information between non-overlapping neuron subsets during activation. High integration in S1/B1 = high IIT fit.

The winning theory is the one with highest mean fit score across all 12 content categories.

### Phase 5 — Contrastive Sub-Study

Run paired stimulus experiments — hold content type constant, vary one semantic dimension, measure activation delta `ΔA = A₂ - A₁`:

| Pair | Fixed | Varied |
|---|---|---|
| Image: neutral vs. emotional | M2 visual content | S3 emotional valence |
| Sentence: declarative vs. narrative | M1 text content | S1 narrative framing |
| Audio: familiar vs. novel | M3 audio content | B4 novelty level |
| Social: low vs. high agency | B1 social content | Theory of mind depth |

`ΔA` isolates the marginal activation contribution of each semantic dimension independently, providing evidence for or against each theory's modular predictions.

### Statistical Rigor

- One-way ANOVA across content type groups for activation magnitude
- Permutation testing (n=10,000) for cluster separability significance
- Bootstrap confidence intervals (95%) on all theory fit scores
- Bonferroni correction for multiple comparisons across 12 content categories
- Effect size reporting (Cohen's d) for all pairwise contrastive comparisons

## 10. Limitations & Threats to Validity

1. **Biological equivalence:** The Tribe model is not a biological brain. Results describe the model's behavior, not human cognition. All neuroscientific theory comparisons are analogical.
2. **Neuron-to-region mapping:** Mapping artificial neurons to brain regions requires explicit heuristic choices that introduce interpretive bias. These choices will be documented and sensitivity-tested.
3. **Dataset coverage:** Real-world datasets may not fully cover all 12 content categories. Gaps filled by synthetic stimuli, which may not capture naturalistic distribution.
4. **IIT Φ computation:** True Φ is NP-hard to compute. The Φ-proxy (mutual information between neuron subsets) is an approximation. Results for IIT fit scores carry higher uncertainty than DCT/GWT/FEP scores.
